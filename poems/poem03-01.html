Returns the middle child, bearing only honor and not a penny to its name. The black sheep of the family. A bystander who has only the present to gamble away, who keeps the mouth shut about the future and steps back. What must it feel like to disappoint people who never expected anything from you?

Separated by a single glass-paneled door, families sit at two dining tables. Outside, the men eat and drink well-prepared food and liquor; inside, the women take the same dishes from the containers and pile them haphazardly onto plates.

Moving back and forth between two tables, drinking alcohol, eating rice, forcing smiles, answering loudly, tucking hair behind my ears and pretending not to know, pretending to seriously ponder what to do next, pretending to be confident, pretending to do my best to make my family proud no matter what, pretending to know what I'm doing now, pretending to have dreams.

Then, clutching a bag stuffed with side dishes, gifts, and pocket money, I boarded the subway and returned to my cramped studio apartment. Leaves fell from the sky like bills. With a few ones I'd pulled out of the ATM instead of depositing, I bought alcohol and painkillers on the way home.
